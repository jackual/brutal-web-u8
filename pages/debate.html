The good

The validity of the design style has been hotly debated, which is to be expected, as, in a sense, the intent of Web Brutalism is to elicit a visceral reaction in the user. Some designers argue that the brash and brazen aesthetic is a much needed wake up call as the internet sleepwalks into a malaise of prosaic homogeny, allowing us to critically engage with and confront the zeitgeist and future of discipline as a whole. It has even been argued that unintuitive and idiosyncratic means of visual communication can ironically force the user to engage fully in order to comprehend the content, allowing for more effective delivery of content.

The bad

However, other practitioners have rejected the notion of Web Brutalism as progress, lambasting it on grounds of accessibility, user experience, inclusivity, and aesthetics. The accessibility of brutalist websites has been criticised, particularly for disabled users and individuals from economically disadvantaged countries who are concerned with the download sizes of pages due to internet speeds and the price of data in relation to wages. However my research suggests that Brutalist websites, especially of the minimalist variety, on average outperform websites from the Alexa Ranking in terms of accessibility and weight (download size).

The ugly

Brutalist websites are subjectively ugly, with research suggesting that attractive interfaces are perceived to be more effective, with users assessing said visual appeal in 50 milliseconds. Choosing this design style could subjectively be seen as a narcissistic endeavour, as the graphic designer pushes their own personal aesthetic at the expense of the user, as if the website were a piece of art. This is reflected in the fact that brutalist websites are disproportionately portfolio websites.
