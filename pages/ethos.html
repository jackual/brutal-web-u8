Architecture

Brutalism is an architectural style that emerged in mid-20th century postwar Britain, characterised by simple, geometric forms, repetitive grids and modules, and raw concrete construction. The term was coined by Swiss-French architect Le Corbusier, deriving from the French béton brut, meaning raw concrete, but also humorously alluding to the poor reception of Brutalism in the UK. Like its architectural counterpart, Web Brutalism adopts geometric rigidity and exposes the 'raw concrete' of web development—the underlying code and markup that are typically hidden beneath layers of stylistic embellishments. This homage extends into the web medium through a design language that values honesty and utilitarianism, stripping away the non-essential to foreground the website’s fundamental structure and purpose.

Minimalist and Non-minimalist

In translating these motifs to the digital realm, Brutalist Web Design manifests primarily in two distinct and seemingly antithetical interpretations, which I will refer to as minimalist and non-minimalist. The minimalist approach focuses on extreme reductionism, consisting of bare-bones HTML and CSS, evoking the totally unadorned and unsentimental nature of Brutalist buildings. Conversely, the non-minimalist approach, reminiscent of opts for a cacophony of haphazardly placed elements, abrasive typography, and harsh colours, actively rebelling against user experience and legibility. Both interpretations share a common disdain for the sleek, polished aesthetics of contemporary web design, yet they diverge in their methods of expressing the core principles of Brutalism.

The web is so boring

design ethos

The ethos of Web Brutalism mirrors the unpretentious and anti-bourgeois values of its architectural namesake. Popular content management systems (CMS) and frameworks like WordPress and Bootstrap, which lead to the recycling of common design patterns and layouts, and design standards set by tech giants, such as Google’s Material Design and Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines, are seen as emblematic of a commercial push towards a standardised, user-friendly web that Web Brutalism inherently resists, harking back to the infancy of web design. UX design is an objective science, not an art form or visual design language, steering design decisions based on qualitative and quantitative data rather than creative impetus. Web Brutalism delights in the opposite—using non-standard navigation and a lack of information hierarchy, essentially turning the web experience into a form of art that challenges the user rather than accommodating them.
