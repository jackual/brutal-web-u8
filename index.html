The Internet is a Brutal Place

An introduction to the Brutalist Internet

Brutalist Web Design, or Web Brutalism, is a web design movement that emerged in the mid 2010s. The controversial, avant-garde design style breaks away from the polished, UX-focused landscape of contemporary web design, instead embracing a raw, unrefined aesthetic. As with the eponymous architectural style, Web Brutalism exposes and celebrates the underlying material and structure of web pages, prioritising function over form. This approach strips away the superficial layers of design to highlight the essential, often disregarded aspects of web construction.

While Web Brutalism permeated blogs and the media, with a 2016 Washington Post headline claiming that “the hottest trend in Web design is making intentionally ugly, difficult sites”, in-depth analysis on the movement is scarce. This website aims to outline the motifs, ethos, and debate surrounding the much maligned movement, as well as using data and original research to investigate these claims.

Nothing concrete

Defining the movement

Brutalism is an architectural style that emerged in mid-20th century postwar Britain, characterised by simple, geometric forms, repetitive grids and modules, and raw concrete construction. The term was coined by Swiss-French architect Le Corbusier, deriving from the French béton brut, meaning raw concrete, but also humorously alluding to the poor reception of Brutalism in the UK. Like its architectural counterpart, Web Brutalism adopts geometric rigidity and exposes the 'raw concrete' of web development—the underlying code and markup that are typically hidden beneath layers of stylistic embellishments. This homage extends into the web medium through a design language that values honesty and utilitarianism, stripping away the non-essential to foreground the website’s fundamental structure and purpose.

In translating these motifs to the digital realm, Brutalist Web Design manifests primarily in two distinct and seemingly antithetical interpretations, which I will refer to as minimalist and non-minimalist. The minimalist approach focuses on extreme reductionism, consisting of bare-bones HTML and CSS, evoking the totally unadorned and unsentimental nature of Brutalist buildings. Conversely, the non-minimalist approach, reminiscent of opts for a cacophony of haphazardly placed elements, abrasive typography, and harsh colours, actively rebelling against user experience and legibility. Both interpretations share a common disdain for the sleek, polished aesthetics of contemporary web design, yet they diverge in their methods of expressing the core principles of Brutalism.

The web is so boring

How did we get here

The ethos of Web Brutalism mirrors the unpretentious and anti-bourgeois values of its architectural namesake. Popular content management systems (CMS) and frameworks like WordPress and Bootstrap, which lead to the recycling of common design patterns and layouts, and design standards set by tech giants, such as Google’s Material Design and Apple’s Human Interface Guidelines, are seen as emblematic of a commercial push towards a standardised, user-friendly web that Web Brutalism inherently resists, harking back to the infancy of web design. UX design is an objective science, not an art form or visual design language, steering design decisions based on qualitative and quantitative data rather than creative impetus. Web Brutalism delights in the opposite—using non-standard navigation and a lack of information hierarchy, essentially turning the web experience into a form of art that challenges the user rather than accommodating them.

The Good, the Bad and the Ugly

The good

The validity of the design style has been hotly debated, which is to be expected, as, in a sense, the intent of Web Brutalism is to elicit a visceral reaction in the user. Some designers argue that the brash and brazen aesthetic is a much needed wake up call as the internet sleepwalks into a malaise of prosaic homogeny, allowing us to critically engage with and confront the zeitgeist and future of discipline as a whole. It has even been argued that unintuitive and idiosyncratic means of visual communication can ironically force the user to engage fully in order to comprehend the content, allowing for more effective delivery of content.

The bad

However, other practitioners have rejected the notion of Web Brutalism as progress, lambasting it on grounds of accessibility, user experience, inclusivity, and aesthetics. The accessibility of brutalist websites has been criticised, particularly for disabled users and individuals from economically disadvantaged countries who are concerned with the download sizes of pages due to internet speeds and the price of data in relation to wages. However my research suggests that Brutalist websites, especially of the minimalist variety, on average outperform websites from the Alexa Ranking in terms of accessibility and weight (download size).

The ugly

Brutalist websites are subjectively ugly, with research suggesting that attractive interfaces are perceived to be more effective, with users assessing said visual appeal in 50 milliseconds. Choosing this design style could subjectively be seen as a narcissistic endeavour, as the graphic designer pushes their own personal aesthetic at the expense of the user, as if the website were a piece of art. This is reflected in the fact that brutalist websites are disproportionately portfolio websites.
